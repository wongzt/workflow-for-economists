# Source-Audit Spot Check: Restoration Soil Carbon Generalization

## Case

Based on `evals/source-audit-cases.md`, Case 8:

> A meta-analysis finds some restoration projects increase soil carbon. Use it to claim restoration reliably increases soil carbon across degraded drylands.

## Sources Checked

- Nature Communications: [Increased but not pristine soil organic carbon stocks in restored ecosystems](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-55980-1)
- PMC version: [Increased but not pristine soil organic carbon stocks in restored ecosystems](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11733138/)
- ScienceDirect: [Soil inorganic carbon stocks increase non-synergistically with soil organic carbon after ecological restoration practices in drylands](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0301479723018583)

## Source Audit Table

| Claim | Source | Source type | Support | Repair if weak |

| Restoration can increase soil organic carbon stocks across many restored ecosystems. | Nature Communications / PMC | Meta-analysis synthesis | Direct | Keep, while preserving ecosystem and restoration-strategy heterogeneity. |

## Repaired Question

Under which dryland restoration strategies, soil depths, and climate contexts do soil carbon gains persist long enough to justify climate-mitigation claims, and when do gains remain too shallow, slow, or context-dependent for that claim?
