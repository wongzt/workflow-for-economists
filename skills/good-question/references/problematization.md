# Problematization

Use this card when the user is scanning literature, looking for a theoretical contribution, or relying on "no one has studied X" as the main argument.

Source: Mats Alvesson and Jorgen Sandberg, "Generating Research Questions Through Problematization", Academy of Management Review, 2011. DOI: https://doi.org/10.5465/amr.2009.0188

## Core Idea

Move beyond gap-spotting. Generate stronger research questions by identifying and challenging assumptions that organize the existing literature.

## Assumption Types

- In-house assumptions: shared within a school, model family, or method tradition.
- Root metaphor assumptions: background analogies that shape what researchers notice.
- Paradigm assumptions: deeper beliefs about valid evidence, explanation, or units of analysis.
- Ideological assumptions: value-laden beliefs about what is desirable or natural.
- Field assumptions: broad consensus across otherwise competing camps.

## Procedure

1. Define the literature domain narrowly enough to inspect.
2. Summarize what the literature usually asks, measures, optimizes, or ignores.
3. Identify 3-5 assumptions behind those choices.
4. Pick one assumption that is important, contestable, and not merely wrong by definition.
5. Invert, relax, historicize, or boundary-test the assumption.
6. Formulate a question that would matter if the assumption failed.

## Prompts

- What must be true for the dominant literature to make sense?
- What is treated as context rather than as an object of explanation?
- Which variable is always controlled away but might be the phenomenon?
- What is assumed stable, universal, beneficial, or measurable?
- What would a different discipline find strange about this literature?

## Red Flags

- The "gap" is only a missing population, country, dataset, or method with no theoretical stake.
- The challenged assumption is too vague to generate evidence.
- The user frames all prior work as naive instead of locating a precise assumption.
- The resulting question cannot be understood by readers of the target literature.
