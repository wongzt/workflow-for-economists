# Fischbach: Problem Picking

Use this card when a researcher is choosing among possible directions, has become method-first, or needs a structured discussion before committing months or years to a project.

Sources:
- Michael A. Fischbach, "Problem choice and decision trees in science and engineering", Cell, 2024. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2024.03.012
- Stanford Engineering, "How to pick and solve the next great problem", 2024. https://engineering.stanford.edu/news/how-pick-and-solve-next-great-problem

## Core Idea

Problem choice is a trainable research skill. Do not rush from an interesting tool, dataset, or fashionable topic into a project. Spend deliberate time making the choice explicit: compare candidate problems, name the decision points, surface traps, and decide what evidence would justify committing.

## Procedure

1. Write 3-5 candidate problems as questions, not as methods or project titles.
2. For each candidate, draw a simple decision tree: if the answer is yes, no, ambiguous, or technically blocked, what would be learned and what would happen next?
3. Name the main trap for each candidate: method-first, easy-but-trivial, fashionable-but-thin, impossible-with-current-access, or high-risk-without-downside-learning.
4. Separate intellectual risk from execution risk. Favor risks that could teach something even when the first hypothesis fails.
5. Pick one next evidence-gathering action: a tiny pilot, a short literature check, a conversation with a domain expert, or a feasibility probe.

## Prompts

- Are we choosing this because it is important, or because the method is already available?
- What would we still learn if the expected answer is wrong?
- Which branch of the decision tree would make us abandon, shrink, or reframe the project?
- What hidden assumption makes this problem look easier than it is?
- What risk is worth taking here, and what risk is merely bad planning?

## Red Flags

- The proposed project starts with a method and only later invents a question.
- The team has not compared alternatives before committing.
- "Feasible" means only that data exists, not that the evidence can answer the question.
- The project has no branch where a negative result is scientifically useful.
- The researcher is avoiding risk so completely that only incremental work remains.
