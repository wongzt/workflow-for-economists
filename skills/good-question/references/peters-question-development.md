# Peters: Question Development

Use this card when the user has a literature cluster, review topic, or vague curiosity and needs to derive a clear research question without prematurely forcing a hypothesis.

Source: Megan Peters, "How to develop good research questions", Nature Human Behaviour, 2025. PDF: https://93649922-4902-4777-bd09-da27e2d86e2f.filesusr.com/ugd/f9caf1_b5e232feb9d044d2aa9f2a342b1604e7.pdf

## Core Idea

Good questions can emerge from disciplined engagement with literature, not only from pre-existing hypotheses. Descriptive, mechanistic, methodological, and normative questions can all be valuable if they remove real uncertainty.

## Procedure

1. Brainstorm questions freely, then critique them for vagueness and assumptions.
2. Build a small literature map: central papers, adjacent papers, methods, constructs, disagreements, and missing links.
3. Close-read the most relevant papers and ask how conclusions depend on measurement, controls, population, analysis, or framing.
4. Reframe questions so they remove a specific uncertainty rather than merely add another study.
5. Decide whether the question is descriptive, causal, mechanistic, methodological, theoretical, or normative.

## Prompts

- What is currently uncertain after reading the strongest papers?
- Which conclusion depends on a design choice that could be varied?
- Which construct is measured indirectly or inconsistently?
- Which alternative explanation is not excluded?
- Would answering this question change how future studies are designed?

## Red Flags

- The user turns every question into a causal hypothesis too early.
- The literature map only includes papers that support the user's preferred idea.
- The question adds a variable but not an uncertainty.
- The answer would not change theory, method, practice, or the next experiment.
