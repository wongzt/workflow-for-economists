# Platt: Strong Inference

Use this card when a question involves mechanisms, causal explanations, model comparison, experimental design, or claims that could be explained by multiple alternatives.

Source: John R. Platt, "Strong Inference", Science, 1964. PDF: https://pages.cs.wisc.edu/~markhill/science64_strong_inference.pdf

## Core Idea

Strong research advances by comparing multiple plausible hypotheses and designing observations or experiments that rule some of them out. Do not let the user defend one favorite hypothesis before alternatives are explicit.

## Procedure

1. State the phenomenon or problem in neutral language.
2. Generate at least three explanations: favored hypothesis, serious rival, and null or artifact.
3. Derive a distinct prediction from each hypothesis.
4. Find a discriminating observation, analysis, or experiment where the hypotheses predict different outcomes.
5. Specify what result would weaken or kill each hypothesis.
6. After the result, branch to the next narrower question.

## Prompts

- What else could explain the same pattern?
- Which explanation would a hostile reviewer prefer?
- What observation would embarrass the favorite hypothesis?
- Can one experiment separate two hypotheses, or only confirm that something happens?
- Is the key result robust to measurement artifact, sampling bias, and baseline choice?

## Red Flags

- Only one hypothesis is being considered.
- The proposed experiment can confirm but not discriminate.
- The null hypothesis is a strawman.
- Outcomes are interpreted after the fact without predeclared predictions.
