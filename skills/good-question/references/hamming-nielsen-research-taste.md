# Hamming and Nielsen: Research Taste

Use this card when the user wants a broad direction, long-term agenda, thesis area, or a way to decide what is worth years of attention.

Sources:
- Richard Hamming, "You and Your Research". https://www.cs.virginia.edu/~robins/YouAndYourResearch
- Michael Nielsen, "Principles of Effective Research". https://michaelnielsen.org/blog/principles-of-effective-research/

## Core Idea

Research taste is trained by repeatedly asking which problems are important, which are attackable now, and which fields are messy enough that a new representation could clarify them.

## Procedure

1. Build an important-problems list for the user's field.
2. Mark which problems are currently attackable by the user's tools, data, and position.
3. Look for messy fields: many facts, weak integration, unstable vocabulary, or repeated empirical surprises.
4. Search for representational shifts: a new measurement, model, analogy, visualization, or dataset that could reorganize the area.
5. Convert broad problems into question families, then into two-week pilots.

## Prompts

- What are the ten most important unsolved problems in this field?
- Which of them can you actually attack from your current position?
- What problem would make your current project matter more if reframed slightly?
- Where does the field have many observations but poor organizing principles?
- Which neighboring discipline has a cleaner representation of a similar phenomenon?
- What skill, tool, or dataset would change your feasible frontier six months from now?

## Red Flags

- The user optimizes for convenience while calling it feasibility.
- The agenda is fashionable but not personally or intellectually durable.
- The field is mature and incremental, with no clear representational opening.
- The idea cannot be connected to a larger important-problems list.
